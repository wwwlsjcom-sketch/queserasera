# Page Density Standard

## Goal

The report should feel like a professional, information-rich consulting deliverable. Visual polish is not enough. Each page should carry meaningful analysis, evidence, implications, and structured visuals.

Use label and chart-text budgets only to prevent overflow. Never use them to reduce analytical depth.

## Page-Level Density Standard

Every major page after the cover should usually include at least three of the following:

- A conclusion-led heading or page thesis.
- 1-3 substantive analytical paragraphs.
- One exhibit, table, scorecard, matrix, map, or structured diagram.
- A management implication, evidence, watch-point, assumption, or caveat panel.
- Source, assumption, methodology, or data-quality note.
- A compact supporting list of drivers, risks, criteria, or decision questions.

Avoid pages that contain only:

- A large heading and a single chart.
- A few metric cards without explanation.
- A table with no interpretation.
- Decorative whitespace that does not improve comprehension.
- Repeated section dividers that create visual drama but little information.

## Recommended Density by Page Type

- Executive summary: thesis paragraph, 3-5 judgment metrics, evidence/implication panel, and at least one compact diagnostic visual when supported.
- Contents: keep compact; include section names plus decision purpose or key question for each section.
- Analytical body page: 2-4 paragraphs plus at least one exhibit and one interpretation panel.
- Data-heavy page: one primary exhibit plus a supporting table, note, or small multiples; explain what changes the conclusion.
- Decision close: recommendation/options, trade-offs, watch points, and decision questions or next analyses.

## Evidence Density

Professional reports should make evidence visible. When the user's material supports it, include:

- Source hierarchy: user-provided data, external research, interviews, assumptions, model outputs.
- Evidence quality: measured, estimated, directional, qualitative, illustrative.
- Decision relevance: why this evidence changes the recommendation.
- Gaps: what remains unknown and how to validate it.

## Exhibit Density

An exhibit should include enough information to stand alone:

- Exhibit number.
- Conclusion-led title.
- Caption explaining the message, data scope, and caveat.
- Axis, legend, labels, or scoring definitions.
- Source or assumption note.
- Nearby implication text that explains "so what."

Do not count raw tables, decorative cards, or one-number tiles as sufficient exhibit diversity.

## Minimum Density Gate

Before delivery, scan every page:

- If a page has fewer than 120 meaningful Chinese characters of analysis and no exhibit, add interpretation or merge it with another page.
- If a page has one exhibit but no explanation, add a narrative paragraph and implication panel.
- If a page has metric cards only, add a driver explanation, source note, or diagnostic visual.
- If a page has a large visual cover-like layout after the cover, reduce whitespace and increase evidence density.
- If the report is data-heavy, include 8-12 exhibits and at least five distinct exhibit types.
